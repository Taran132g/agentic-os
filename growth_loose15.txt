There's a quiet fear you carry into every room. That one day, everyone's going to figure out you don't actually know what you're doing.
You got the grade. The job. The compliment. And instead of feeling proud, you felt like you got away with something. Like there's a clock ticking until you're exposed.
So you over-prepare. You over-work. You never raise your hand unless you're completely sure, because being wrong feels like proof of the fraud you're convinced you are.
Here's what nobody tells you. That feeling isn't evidence that you're a fake. It's evidence you're in a room that matters to you, doing something you actually care about getting right.
And the people you're terrified of disappointing? Half of them feel the exact same way. Each one sure everyone else got a manual they never received.
Confidence was never the absence of that fear. It's doing the thing while the fear talks, and slowly noticing the world doesn't end.
You don't beat this by finally feeling qualified enough. You beat it by letting yourself be seen trying, before you feel ready.
Ask the question. Send the work. Say "I'm not totally sure, but here's my best shot."
You're not a fraud who got lucky. You're someone growing so fast your self-image just hasn't caught up yet.
